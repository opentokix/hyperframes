# Visual Motifs

A motif is a **recurring visual metaphor** that threads through the entire video — from hook to CTA. It gives the video a signature look.

## State-Change Axis

Every motif has a **before → after** axis. Pick ONE axis and commit to it for the entire video:

- Problem ↔ Solution
- Empty ↔ Full
- Disconnected ↔ Connected
- Broken ↔ Fixed
- Slow ↔ Fast
- Complex ↔ Simple
- Hidden ↔ Visible

The hook scene shows the "before" state; the CTA scene shows the "after" state. Middle scenes animate the transition.

## Color Script

Colors shift across scenes to reinforce the narrative arc:

1. **Hook** — neutral warm palette (establish mood, don't distract from text)
2. **Problem** — cool tones or desaturated (tension, something's wrong)
3. **Solution** — brand primary, full saturation (the payoff)
4. **CTA** — warm, inviting, high contrast (action)

## Kinetic Type Restraint

- Max 2 animated text elements per scene
- No more than 1 text animation style per scene (e.g., don't mix slide-up AND typewriter)
- Text animation duration ≤ 0.8s
- Prefer opacity + translateY over complex transforms

## First 10 Seconds

The value proposition must be **visually evident** within the first 10 seconds (≈30% of a 30s video). "Evident" means:

- The viewer can articulate what the product does
- At least one concrete example has appeared
- The motif has been introduced

## Dev-Tool Aesthetic

For developer-facing videos:

- Dark backgrounds (`#0A0A0A` to `#1A1A1A`)
- Monospace code with syntax highlighting
- Terminal-style UI elements
- Subtle grid or dot patterns
- Avoid stock photography, gradients-for-the-sake-of-gradients, corporate blue

## Generic Motifs to Avoid

These are overused and signal "template video":

- Rocket ship (for anything)
- Light bulb (for ideas)
- Puzzle pieces (for integration)
- Gears (for process)
- Globe (for global/scale)

Instead, derive a motif from the **specific verb** of what the product does.
